💡 Why Does the Coil Produce a Sine Wave?

The coil rotates at constant speed ω between the N and S poles. The key is how fast the coil cuts through field lines:

  • θ = 0° — Coil faces field head-on → zero cutting → EMF = 0
  • θ = 90° — Coil edge slices through field → max cutting → EMF = peak
  • θ = 180° — Faces field again → zero cutting → EMF = 0
  • θ = 270° — Max cutting, opposite direction → EMF = −peak
e(t) = Vm · sin(ωt)  ← this is why it's a sine!
📐 Step-by-Step Mathematical Derivation
1Magnetic Flux Through the Coil

A coil of N turns, area A rotates in a uniform field B. At angle θ = ωt from the field direction:

Φ = N · B · A · cos(ωt)

When the coil is perpendicular to B, Φ is maximum. When parallel, Φ = 0.

2Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

The induced EMF is the negative rate of change of flux over time:

e = −dΦ/dt

Differentiating the flux equation:

d/dt [NBA · cos(ωt)] = −NBAω · sin(ωt)

With the negative sign from Faraday's law cancelling:

e = NBAω · sin(ωt)
3Defining Peak EMF (Vm)

The term NBAω is a constant — the maximum possible EMF:

Vm = N · B · A · ω

So the final equation becomes:

e(t) = Vm · sin(ωt)
4Adding Phase Shift

If the coil starts at any arbitrary angle φ (phase shift):

e(t) = Vm · sin(ωt + φ)
5Angular Frequency

ω relates to frequency f and period T as:

ω = 2πf = 2π/T   (rad/s)

🎯 Key Physical Insights
  • At θ = 0° — coil perpendicular to B → Φ is max → e = 0 (no cutting)
  • At θ = 90° — coil parallel to B → Φ = 0 → e = Vm (max cutting)
  • At θ = 180° — same as 0° → e = 0 again
  • At θ = 270° — max cutting, opposite direction → e = −Vm
  • This is why AC current reverses direction every half cycle

⚡ Core EMF Formulas

Instantaneous EMF:

e(t) = Vm · sin(ωt + φ)

Peak / Maximum EMF:

Vm = N · B · A · ω

Angular Frequency:

ω = 2πf = 2π / T

Flux (Faraday Source):

Φ = N · B · A · cos(ωt)

Faraday's Law:

e = −dΦ/dt
📐 RMS, Average & Form Factor

RMS Voltage (equivalent DC):

Vrms = Vm / √2 ≈ 0.707 · Vm

Average Voltage (half-cycle):

Vavg = 2Vm / π ≈ 0.637 · Vm

Peak Factor (Crest Factor):

Kp = Vm / Vrms = √2 ≈ 1.414

Form Factor:

Kf = Vrms / Vavg = π/(2√2) ≈ 1.111

RMS Current:

Irms = Im / √2
💡 Power Formulas

Instantaneous Power:

p(t) = v(t) · i(t)

Real (Active) Power:

P = Vrms · Irms · cos(θ) [W]

Reactive Power:

Q = Vrms · Irms · sin(θ) [VAR]

Apparent Power:

S = Vrms · Irms [VA]

Power Factor:

PF = cos(θ) = P / S

Energy Consumed:

W = P · t [Joules]
🔌 Impedance (AC Circuits)

Ohm's Law for AC:

V = I · Z

Total Impedance (RLC):

Z = √(R² + (XL − XC)²)

Inductive Reactance:

XL = ω · L = 2πf · L [Ω]

Capacitive Reactance:

XC = 1 / (ω · C) = 1/(2πfC) [Ω]

Phase Angle:

θ = arctan((XL − XC) / R)
🎵 Resonance & Bandwidth

Resonant Frequency:

f₀ = 1 / (2π√LC) [Hz]

At Resonance (XL = XC):

Z_min = R, PF = 1, P = max

Quality Factor:

Q_factor = ω₀L / R = 1/(ω₀CR)

Bandwidth:

BW = f₀ / Q_factor [Hz]

Half-power frequencies:

f₁, f₂ = f₀ ± BW/2
🔁 Transformer Formulas

Turns Ratio:

a = N₁ / N₂ = V₁ / V₂

Voltage Transformation:

V₂ = V₁ · (N₂ / N₁)

Current Transformation:

I₂ = I₁ · (N₁ / N₂)

Power Conservation (ideal):

V₁ · I₁ = V₂ · I₂

Efficiency:

η = Pout / Pin × 100%

🏭 Why AC Won the "War of Currents"

In the 1880s, Nikola Tesla (AC) and Thomas Edison (DC) competed to establish the dominant electrical standard.

AC won because of one critical formula:

P_loss = I² · R

By stepping voltage up with a transformer, current decreases proportionally, dramatically reducing power losses over long transmission lines. DC had no efficient way to do this at the time.

  • AC can travel hundreds of kilometres efficiently
  • DC power stations required a station every mile
  • Today, HVDC (High Voltage DC) is used for very long submarine cables
